Overview — Stem Cells & Differentiation

Stem cells are undifferentiated cells capable of self-renewal and differentiation into specialised cell types. Differentiation involves the expression of specific genes to produce proteins characteristic of a particular cell type.

1. Types of Stem Cells

  • Totipotent: can form all cell types including extra-embryonic tissues (zygote and early divisions)
  • Pluripotent: can differentiate into any cell type of the body (e.g., embryonic stem cells)
  • Multipotent: can differentiate into a limited range of cell types (e.g., haematopoietic stem cells)
  • Unipotent: only one cell type, but retains ability to self-renew (e.g., muscle stem cells)

2. Differentiation

  • Process by which cells become specialised for structure and function
  • Controlled by gene expression, transcription factors, and epigenetic modifications
  • Cells gain unique organelles, enzymes, and surface markers specific to their role

3. Stem Cells in Medicine

  • Treatment of diseases (e.g., leukaemia via bone marrow transplants)
  • Tissue engineering and regenerative medicine
  • Potential use in organ repair and cell replacement therapies

4. OCR focus — exam points

  • Define stem cells, totipotent, pluripotent, multipotent
  • Explain differences between mitotic division and differentiation
  • Discuss therapeutic uses and ethical considerations
  • Recognise examples of tissues maintained by stem cells
Quick exam hints
  • Memorise types of stem cells and their potency
  • Describe gene expression as mechanism for differentiation
  • Give specific medical examples (bone marrow, skin grafts)
  • Understand ethical debate points for stem cell use

5. Experimental context

  • Observation of stem cell cultures under microscope
  • Tracking differentiation in vitro via marker proteins
  • Compare totipotent, pluripotent, and multipotent in experiments
