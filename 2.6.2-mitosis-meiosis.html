Overview — Mitosis & Meiosis

Mitosis and meiosis are two forms of nuclear division that allow eukaryotic cells to proliferate and generate gametes, respectively. Both processes are tightly regulated to ensure genomic integrity.

1. Mitosis

  • Purpose: growth, tissue repair, asexual reproduction
  • Phases:
    • Prophase: Chromosomes condense, spindle forms, nuclear envelope breaks down
    • Metaphase: Chromosomes align at metaphase plate
    • Anaphase: Sister chromatids separate to opposite poles
    • Telophase: Nuclear envelope reforms, chromosomes decondense
    • Cytokinesis: Cytoplasm divides to form two daughter cells
  • Produces two genetically identical diploid cells
  • Used to maintain chromosome number across somatic cells

2. Meiosis

  • Purpose: sexual reproduction, generate genetic diversity in gametes
  • Two successive divisions:
    • Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes separate (reduction division), crossing-over occurs
    • Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate, similar to mitosis
  • Produces four genetically unique haploid cells
  • Independent assortment and recombination increase genetic variation

3. Key differences between mitosis & meiosis

  • Mitosis: 1 division → 2 identical diploid cells; Meiosis: 2 divisions → 4 unique haploid cells
  • Crossing-over occurs only in meiosis I
  • Meiosis introduces genetic variation; mitosis maintains genomic stability
  • Both have checkpoints: G₁, G₂, spindle assembly (M)

4. OCR focus — exam points

  • Describe stages clearly with chromatid/chromosome terminology
  • Explain genetic variation mechanisms: independent assortment, crossing-over, random fertilization
  • Relate errors (non-disjunction, aneuploidy) to human conditions (e.g., Down syndrome)
  • Link to cell cycle checkpoints and cancer for mitosis questions
Quick exam hints
  • Always specify ploidy: diploid/haploid
  • Label chromatids, centromeres, homologous chromosomes in diagrams
  • Mitosis = somatic cells, Meiosis = gametes
  • Crossing-over occurs in prophase I of meiosis

5. Experimental context

  • Observe mitosis in root tip squashes using stains (e.g., aceto-orcein) and calculate mitotic index
  • Use microscopes to identify stages and number of dividing cells
  • Diagram comparisons of mitosis vs meiosis for clarity
