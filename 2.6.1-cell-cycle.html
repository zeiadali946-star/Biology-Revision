Overview — The Cell Cycle

The cell cycle describes the ordered series of events by which a cell grows and divides to produce two genetically identical daughter cells. It is split into interphase (preparatory phases) and the mitotic phase (mitosis and cytokinesis). Precise control at checkpoints ensures correct DNA replication and division — failure to control the cycle can lead to mutations or uncontrolled growth (cancer).

1. Phases of the Cell Cycle (overview)

  • G₁ (First gap) — cell grows, produces RNA, proteins and organelles; key decision point (G₁ checkpoint) to continue dividing or enter G₀ (rest).
  • S (Synthesis) — DNA replication (each chromosome duplicated to form sister chromatids joined at the centromere); centrosomes duplicate in animal cells.
  • G₂ (Second gap) — further growth and preparation for mitosis; DNA repair systems check for replication errors; G₂ checkpoint ensures all DNA is replicated and undamaged.
  • M (Mitosis) — nuclear division (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) followed by cytokinesis (cytoplasm divides) producing two daughter cells.

2. Interphase detail

Interphase (G₁ → S → G₂) typically occupies the majority of the cycle. DNA is not condensed into visible chromosomes during interphase; instead it exists as chromatin. Key processes:

  • Replication origins open and replication forks proceed bidirectionally during S phase.
  • DNA repair pathways (e.g., mismatch repair) act during and after replication.
  • Cell assesses nutrient status and external signals (growth factors) in G₁ to decide whether to proliferate.

3. Checkpoints — control points

Checkpoints verify whether the processes at each phase are complete and if the cell is ready to proceed.

  • G₁ checkpoint (Restriction point): checks cell size, nutrients, growth factors, and DNA damage. Passage commits the cell to DNA replication.
  • G₂ checkpoint: assesses DNA replication completeness and repair of damage before mitosis.
  • Spindle (M) checkpoint: ensures all chromosomes are properly attached to the spindle before allowing anaphase (separation of sister chromatids).

4. Molecular regulation: Cyclins & CDKs

The cell cycle is regulated by cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs) and their regulatory subunits — cyclins. Cyclin levels vary during the cycle, binding to CDKs to form active complexes that phosphorylate target proteins to drive progression.

  • Cyclin D (G₁): regulated by growth factors; promotes G₁ → S transition.
  • Cyclin E (late G₁) & Cyclin A (S): required for initiation and progression of DNA replication.
  • Cyclin B (G₂/M): activates CDK1 to trigger entry into mitosis (MPF — maturation/mitosis-promoting factor).

5. Checkpoint mechanisms & tumour suppression

Proteins like p53 and Rb are crucial checkpoint controllers. p53 senses DNA damage and can: halt the cycle (allow repair), trigger senescence, or initiate apoptosis if damage is irreparable. Mutations in checkpoint genes (e.g., p53) are common in cancer.

6. DNA damage response & apoptosis

  • When damage is detected, ATM/ATR kinases activate signalling cascades that stabilise p53 and activate repair genes.
  • If repair fails, p53 can activate pro-apoptotic pathways (e.g., BAX/BAK) leading to programmed cell death — this prevents propagation of harmful mutations.
Quick facts & exam hints
  • Describe G₁, S, G₂ and M when asked for stages of the cell cycle.
  • For control questions, always mention cyclins, CDKs and p53 as core regulators.
  • For diagrams — label replication (S) and checkpoints; MPF = cyclin B + CDK1.
  • Link errors in checkpoint control to uncontrolled mitosis/cancer in longer-answer questions.

7. Mitosis vs Meiosis — brief link

Mitosis produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells (used for growth, repair and asexual reproduction). Meiosis produces four genetically varied haploid gametes (sexual reproduction) — meiosis involves two successive divisions (meiosis I & II) and includes events such as crossing-over and independent assortment (covered in detail in 2.6.2/2.6.3 pages).

8. Practical & experimental contexts (OCR focus)

  • Cell cycle stages can be observed using staining and light microscopy (e.g., root tip squash to score mitotic index = fraction of cells in mitosis).
  • Flow cytometry measures DNA content to distinguish G₀/G₁, S and G₂/M populations.
  • Drugs that inhibit cell cycle are used clinically (e.g., chemotherapy agents targeting rapidly dividing cells — microtubule inhibitors like vincristine/vinblastine disrupt mitotic spindle).

9. Common exam question prompts & how to answer

  • “Describe the stages of the cell cycle” — give G₁, S, G₂, M with one or two key molecular events each.
  • “Explain how the cell cycle is controlled” — discuss cyclins, CDKs, checkpoints, p53 and how external signals (growth factors) modulate cyclin expression.
  • “Suggest why loss of function in p53 can cause cancer” — explain failed DNA-damage response, failure to initiate apoptosis, accumulation of mutations leading to uncontrolled division.
